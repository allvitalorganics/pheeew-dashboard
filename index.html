This is the financial model behind our angel round — the numbers and plan that back the pitch deck. It's long (21 sections) because it's internal working material, not the pitch deck itself. You don't need to read every word. Read the parts that matter for the questions you care about.

  • 5 minutes → Read Section 01 (Executive Summary) + Key Takeaways at the end. That's the whole ask in two pieces.
  • 20 minutes → Add Section 06 (Plan), Section 08 (Budget), Section 16 (Valuation), Section 17 (Deal). Now you understand the what, how much, and why that price.
  • 45 minutes → All of it, in order. You'll be able to answer any question Jason's father asks.

How to navigate

  • Every section starts with a one-line TL;DR — read that first to decide if you need the detail.
  • Colored boxes tell you the signal: accent (brown) = our interpretation · green = positive fact · yellow = watch-out · red = risk or constraint · blue = strategic note.
  • Tables show source data. Callouts underneath tell you what it means.
  • Bottom-right corner has a ↑ button — use it to jump back to the top of any section.

Quick glossary (if any of these throw you off)

Pre-money valuation — what the company is worth before new money comes in. Ours is $1.65M.
Post-money valuation — pre-money + investment. Ours is $1.805M.
Cap table — the spreadsheet showing who owns what percentage of the company.
Dilution — when new shares are issued, everyone's % gets a bit smaller (but the whole pie is bigger).
MOQ — Minimum Order Quantity. Our factory's minimum run is 40,000 pouches per flavor.
COGS — Cost of Goods Sold. What it costs us to produce one pouch, landed in LA: $1.05.
Gross margin — profit % after COGS. Ours is 50.6% — healthy for CPG.
Runway — how long the money lasts before we need more. This round = 9 months.
Priced round — explicit valuation, explicit equity %, standard common stock. Clean for a CFO investor.
SAFE — Simple Agreement for Future Equity. Defers the valuation conversation to the next round. Common in US startups.
Advisor shares — equity granted to an advisor (not an investor). Vests over time to keep them engaged.
Seed round — the round after this one. Typically $1.5M–$2.5M, from institutional seed funds.

One more thing before you dive in

This document is designed so a reader with zero context about the company could understand the ask. That means a lot of it will feel like stuff you already know. Skim those parts. 06Go-to-Market — Commit + Parallel Market Test

TL;DRThe plan. Commit 40K factory order Week 1. Run farmer's markets with sample pouches during the 3–4 month production wait. Full DTC launch when main inventory lands.

A conservative approach produces in parallel with market validation — not after. We commit the 40K MOQ at funding close so production runs during the 3–4 month lead time, while the team is on-the-ground at LA farmer's markets with pre-production samples, gathering real customer signal before the big inventory lands.

Why parallel (commit + test) instead of sequential (test then commit). Production lead time at 좋은영농 is 3 months + 1–2 months shipping. If we test first and only commit after validation, launch slips 4–5 months — missing the Aug–Sep 2026 target window Jason's father may remember from prior conversations. Parallel deployment: test learnings inform launch positioning without delaying the inventory.

15Brand Lifetime — Community as Moat

TL;DRWhy Pheeew outlasts typical CPG brands. Average CPG brand lifecycle has shrunk from 6–10 years to 3–5. Pheeew is designed so the community — not the founder — is the moat.

Average CPG brand lifecycle has compressed from 6–10 years (pre-2015) to 3–5 years (post-2020). Brand differentiation commoditizes faster; attention is stateless. Pheeew is designed to beat that curve by building community as structural moat.
